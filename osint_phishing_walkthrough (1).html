Before You Begin

What is a Phishing Campaign Investigation?

When a suspicious email lands in an inbox — whether it's a spoofed sender, a malicious link, or an unusual attachment — someone needs to determine if it's a real threat. This walkthrough gives you a repeatable, tiered process using free OSINT (Open Source Intelligence) tools to go from first look to full threat attribution. Each tier builds on the last, and each tool has a clear "when to escalate" trigger.

🎯 Who This Is For

SOC analysts, IT administrators, help desk staff, and security-curious professionals who need to triage phishing reports efficiently without paid tooling.

⚠️ Key Terminology

IOC — Indicator of Compromise. Any artifact (IP, domain, hash) that signals malicious activity.
TTP — Tactics, Techniques & Procedures. The "how" of an attacker.
Spoofing — Faking a sender identity to deceive recipients.

📋 What You'll Need

The raw email headers (copy from your email client), the sender's domain and/or IP address, any URLs or attachments included in the suspicious message.

🔴 Safety First

Never click links directly from suspicious emails. Copy URLs by right-clicking. Never open attachments — hash them first. Use a sandboxed browser or VM when possible.

01

TIER 1 — TRIAGE & SURFACE ANALYSIS

INITIAL ASSESSMENT · PERFORMED BY L1 SOC ANALYST OR HELP DESK

Tier 1 is your first line of defense. The goal here is rapid triage: determine in under 10 minutes whether a reported email is suspicious or benign, and flag the key IOCs for deeper review. These tools require no installation and no accounts (for basic use).

📧 Google Admin Toolbox — Email Header Analyzer

toolbox.googleapps.com/apps/messageheader
TIER 1 NO ACCOUNT NEEDED

Every email carries invisible metadata called email headers — a chain of server hops, timestamps, and authentication results that tell the real story of how a message got to you. The Google Admin Toolbox parses these headers visually, surfacing authentication failures and suspicious routing in seconds. Think of it as the email's "flight record."

1

In your email client (Gmail, Outlook, etc.), open the suspicious email and access Show Original or View Raw Headers. Copy the entire header block.

2

Navigate to the tool URL, paste the header text into the input field, and click Analyze the header above.

3

Review the delivery chain. Look for: SPF: FAIL, DKIM: FAIL, DMARC: FAIL — any failure is a red flag indicating the sender domain may be spoofed.

4

Note the originating IP address from the Received: from field — copy this for MXToolbox investigation in the next step.

💡 Analyst Note SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are three authentication protocols that verify a sender's identity. If all three fail together, the email is almost certainly spoofed. A mismatched Return-Path domain (different from the From address) is another classic spoofing indicator.
⬆ When to Escalate to Tier 2

Escalate if: (1) SPF/DKIM/DMARC all fail, (2) the originating IP is in a foreign/unexpected country, (3) the Return-Path differs from the From address, or (4) multiple users reported the same email. Document the originating IP and sender domain before escalating.

🔍 MXToolbox — DNS & Blacklist Lookup

mxtoolbox.com
TIER 1 FREE TIER AVAILABLE

MXToolbox is a Swiss Army knife for DNS and email infrastructure analysis. It lets you look up MX records (which servers handle email for a domain), check if an IP or domain is on any known blacklists, verify SPF/DMARC records, and perform WHOIS lookups — all from a single interface. If an IP from the header analysis is blacklisted, that's your first hard IOC.

1

Go to mxtoolbox.com and paste the sender's domain (e.g., paypa1-secure.com) into the search bar. Run a MX Lookup first to see if mail servers exist at all — fake domains often have none.

2

Run a Blacklist Check on the originating IP address from the headers. MXToolbox checks against 100+ real-time blacklists simultaneously.

3

Use SPF Record Lookup and DMARC Lookup to verify whether the domain even has these policies configured. No DMARC record means the domain owner has no enforcement in place — attackers exploit this.

4

Run a WHOIS lookup on the domain. Note the registration date — domains registered within the last 30 days are a major red flag (known as "young domains").

⬆ When to Escalate to Tier 2

Escalate if: (1) IP hits 2+ major blacklists, (2) the sender domain was registered within the last 30 days, (3) no legitimate MX records exist for the claimed organization's domain, or (4) WHOIS privacy is concealing the registrant and the domain mimics a known brand (typosquatting). Proceed to VirusTotal for URL and attachment analysis.

02

TIER 2 — DEEP LINK & PAYLOAD ANALYSIS

ARTIFACT EXAMINATION · PERFORMED BY L2 SOC ANALYST

Tier 2 moves beyond metadata into the actual payloads: the URLs embedded in the email and any attached files. At this level you're confirming malicious intent and gathering IOCs for blocking and reporting. This is where most SOC investigations either close (block + report) or escalate further.

🛡 VirusTotal — URL, File & Hash Scanner

virustotal.com
TIER 2 FREE · ACCOUNT OPTIONAL

VirusTotal aggregates 70+ antivirus engines and threat intelligence feeds into a single scan. You can submit a URL, a file, or an MD5/SHA256 hash and receive a verdict from the entire security industry at once. Crucially, do not click links — paste the URL directly into VirusTotal. For attachments, generate a hash first using a tool like CertUtil (Windows) or sha256sum (Linux/Mac) and submit the hash only.

1

Right-click any link in the suspicious email and copy the URL without clicking it. Navigate to virustotal.com and paste it into the URL tab.

2

For attachments: on Windows, open Command Prompt and run certutil -hashfile attachment.pdf SHA256 to generate a hash. Submit only the hash string to VirusTotal — never upload sensitive files.

3

Review the Detection tab. Any detection count above 3/72 is worth acting on. Look at the Community Score and the Relations tab for connected domains and IPs.

4

Check the Details tab for the URL's HTTP response, final redirect destination, and any dropped files. Phishing pages often redirect multiple times before landing.

⬆ When to Escalate to Tier 3

Escalate if: (1) URL scores 5+ detections with "Phishing" or "Trojan" labels, (2) the Relations tab shows connections to known C2 (command-and-control) infrastructure, (3) a file hash has behavioral detections even with low AV scores, or (4) the attack appears targeted (spear-phishing) against a specific person or role in the organization. At this point, pattern analysis and threat actor attribution become necessary.

🌐 URLScan.io — Live URL & Page Inspector

urlscan.io
TIER 2 FREE · ACCOUNT OPTIONAL

URLScan.io safely "visits" a URL in an isolated browser sandbox and captures a screenshot, the full DOM, network requests, redirects, and all resources loaded — without you ever touching the page. This is invaluable for analyzing credential-harvesting pages, fake login portals, and drive-by download sites while keeping your machine completely safe.

1

Paste the suspicious URL into urlscan.io and submit a public scan. Scans are typically complete in 20–30 seconds.

2

Review the Screenshot tab — you'll see exactly what a victim would see. Fake login pages for PayPal, Microsoft, or banks are visually obvious here.

3

Check the HTTP tab for redirect chains. A phishing URL often redirects through 3–5 hops to avoid detection. Each hop is a potential IOC.

4

Use the DOM tab to look for form action URLs — where are stolen credentials being sent? This is often a different server than the page itself.

⬆ When to Escalate to Tier 3

Escalate if: the form action sends credentials to a third-party domain suggesting an organized infrastructure, multiple redirectors are being used (indicating kit-based phishing), or the page is mimicking an internal corporate tool (suggesting targeted insider-threat scenarios). At this point, Maltego is used to map the full infrastructure.

03

TIER 3 — THREAT ACTOR & CAMPAIGN MAPPING

ATTRIBUTION & INTELLIGENCE · PERFORMED BY L3 ANALYST / THREAT INTEL TEAM

Tier 3 shifts the mission from "is this bad?" to "who is doing this, how broadly, and what do we do about it?" This is where you connect individual IOCs into an interconnected threat picture — mapping infrastructure, identifying campaign patterns, and producing intelligence that can protect your entire organization and be shared with the wider security community.

🕸 Maltego CE — Infrastructure Graph Intelligence

maltego.com/maltego-community
TIER 3 FREE COMMUNITY EDITION

Maltego is a visual link-analysis platform that transforms a single IOC — an IP address, domain, or email — into a full relationship graph by querying dozens of data sources simultaneously. The Community Edition (CE) is free and supports the core transforms needed for phishing campaign mapping. Unlike the previous tools, Maltego requires a free account registration and desktop installation (~150MB).

1

Install Maltego CE from the official site, create a free account, and launch the application. Select New Graph and drag an IP Address entity onto the canvas.

2

Enter the malicious IP from your Tier 1 investigation. Right-click the node and run All Transforms → To DNS Name to discover all domains hosted on that IP.

3

From any discovered domain, run To WHOIS — Registrant Email. If the threat actor reused an email across registrations, this exposes their entire portfolio of malicious domains.

4

Run To IP Address [DNS] from each domain, then To Netblock [Owner] to identify the hosting ASN (Autonomous System Number) — which hosting provider is sheltering this infrastructure.

5

Export the graph as a PNG or CSV for your incident report. Highlight nodes in red that represent confirmed malicious infrastructure.

🎣 PhishTank & OpenPhish — Phishing Campaign Databases

phishtank.org openphish.com
TIER 3 FREE · COMMUNITY-DRIVEN

PhishTank and OpenPhish are crowd-sourced and automated databases of known phishing URLs, updated in real time. At Tier 3, you use these to determine if your discovered URLs are part of a broader, already-documented campaign — and to submit new URLs so others can be protected. Think of them as community threat-sharing platforms.

1

On phishtank.org, use the Check a URL feature to query any suspicious URL. The result tells you if the URL has been previously verified by community analysts as phishing.

2

If the URL is not in PhishTank yet but your analysis confirms it's malicious, submit it using Add a Phish. This protects every organization using PhishTank as a blocklist feed.

3

Cross-reference with openphish.com/feed.txt — OpenPhish provides a raw text feed of active phishing URLs that you can ingest into a SIEM or firewall rule as an automated blocklist.

⬆ When to Involve External Parties

At Tier 3, escalation changes character. If your analysis confirms a novel, targeted campaign: (1) Report to CISA (cisa.gov/reporting) if you're in critical infrastructure. (2) File with the Anti-Phishing Working Group at reportphishing@apwg.org. (3) If a real brand is being spoofed (PayPal, your bank, Microsoft), notify their abuse team — most have dedicated security emails. (4) Preserve all evidence with timestamps for potential legal proceedings.

Reference

Key Terms Glossary

SPF (Sender Policy Framework)
A DNS record listing which mail servers are authorized to send email for a domain. A "FAIL" means the sending server isn't on the approved list.
DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)
A cryptographic signature in the email header that proves the message wasn't tampered with in transit. A "FAIL" means the signature is invalid or missing.
DMARC
A policy that tells receiving servers what to do when SPF or DKIM fails (reject, quarantine, or do nothing). Many organizations leave this unconfigured — a gap attackers exploit.
IOC (Indicator of Compromise)
A technical artifact — IP, domain, URL, file hash — that signals malicious activity. IOCs are used to block threats and search for related activity in logs.
Typosquatting
Registering a domain that closely resembles a trusted brand — like "paypa1.com" vs "paypal.com" — to deceive inattentive users.
ASN (Autonomous System Number)
An identifier for a network operated by a single organization (like an ISP or hosting provider). Malicious actors often cluster on low-cost, permissive ASNs.
C2 (Command and Control)
Infrastructure used by attackers to communicate with compromised systems. A malware-infected machine "phones home" to a C2 server to receive instructions.
Spear Phishing
A targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization, using personalized information to increase credibility. More dangerous than generic phishing.
OSINT (Open Source Intelligence)
Intelligence gathered from publicly available sources — DNS records, WHOIS, social media, leaked databases, etc. — without requiring privileged access.
SHA256 Hash
A unique 64-character fingerprint of a file. If two files have the same hash, they are identical. Used to identify malware without running it.
SIEM
Security Information and Event Management platform — a centralized system that ingests logs and alerts from across an organization for correlation and detection.
Pivot
In OSINT, to use one known piece of information (an IP, email, or domain) as a starting point to discover related, previously unknown infrastructure or actors.
